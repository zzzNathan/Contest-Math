\textit{"All constructive problems are trivial."}

\begin{problem}{Mathematical Reflections --- S705}
Prove that there are infinitely many triples $(a,b,c)$ of distinct positive
integers such that $a^3 + b^3 + c^3 - 3abc$ is a perfect square.
\end{problem}

\begin{solution}{Solution}
We make use of the well-known identity

$$
a^3 + b^3 + c^3 - 3abc = \frac{1}{2}(a + b + c)\left[ (a-b)^2 +
(b-c)^2 + (c-a)^2 \right]
$$

Let $c < b < a$ be in arithmetic progression with common difference
$d > 1$, where $d$ is a positive integer. We have

\[
\begin{aligned}
a^3 + b^3 + c^3 - 3abc &= \frac{1}{2} (c + 3d) \left[ d^2 + d^2 + 4d^2 \right]\\
&= 3d^2 (c + 3d) \\
\end{aligned}
\]

Now let $c = 3d^2 - 3d = 3d(d - 1)$ and we have $a^3 + b^3 + c^3 - 3abc =
\left(3d^2\right)^2$
\end{solution}
